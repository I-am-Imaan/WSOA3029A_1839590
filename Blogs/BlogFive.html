Close Reading On Knaflic 'Chapter 6'

Data visualizations are a means of being able to communicate and convey specific sets of data and information to a specific target audience. In the process of creating data visualizations, it is important to take into consideration how exactly it is intended for an audience to process and receive the data information (i.e., identifying what information needs to be emphasised, highlighted, elaborated on, etc., in terms of importance). There are several factors that can be taken into account and improved on, in order to create the best possible data visualization – catering for and informing a specific target audience. These factors will be pointed out, analysed, and discussed with regard to Chapter 6 of the Knaflic reading, titled ‘Dissecting model visuals’ – in which Knaflic has provided numerous data visualisations that have been well designed and well catered to their specific target audiences. In these data visualisations, factors such as the use of colour, line thickness, font type and style, text choice, positioning, placement, alignment etc., have all been taken into account and used to enhance the information presented within each data visualisation. These factors will be further discussed and analysed below.

The colour, thickness and styling of certain aspects within data visualizations, play an important role in allowing an audience to be able to identify, read and retain important information provided through the data visualization. Using multiple different colours within a data visualization, will confuse the audience and make it hard to identify what information is important, as well as make it hard to understand the data visualization in the first place. Displaying information, within a data visualization, in different shades of greyscale and using shades of one other colour (e.g., shades of blue) will allow for information in the other colour to be emphasised within the data visualization, thereby highlighting it as important. This emphasis of importance can also be achieved through making a line, bar, text, figure, etc. thicker within a data visualization, or styling it differently in comparison to the rest of the data present within a data visualization (i.e., using dotted lines or different shading etc.).

The text used within a data visualization is also important in communicating information to an audience. Text can be used through the title of a data visualization, labelling of axes and graphs themselves, as well as through annotations provided within data visualizations. All text found and displayed within data visualizations should be chosen and worded appropriately. Only important and crucial information should be displayed, in order to aid the audiences understanding of the information presented. The usage of text should be kept to a minimal, again only highlighting important information. Text should be well considered and placed in a way that does not clutter the data visualization and make it hard to read and understand for the audience. Preferably, text should always be justified or left aligned, as well as horizontally displayed on a data visualization – this makes it easier to read and process the information provided through the text.

When using numerical figures within a data visualisation, it is important to carefully consider how these figures are displayed. Figures should be rounded off where possible, not be too long, and if they can be provided to the audience in intervals, they should be. Good data visualizations are designed to be scannable, highlighting important information and making it easily identifiable to an audience. The choice of data visualization type, as well as how all of the above factors are utilized and implemented, is also dependent on the type of audience, as well as personal preference of the designer. However, the use of certain conventions and styles are recommended, in order to cater to a larger target audience, as well as make the data information more universal.

References
